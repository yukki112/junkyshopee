Remove Labels Guide

Increase your recycling value by properly removing labels and contaminants

Non-recyclable labels and adhesives can contaminate recycling streams and reduce material quality. Proper label removal helps ensure your recyclables maintain maximum value at junkshops.

Why Label Removal Matters

Labels made from different materials can contaminate recycling batches. Adhesives can gum up recycling machinery. Clean, label-free materials are easier to process and often qualify for higher quality (and higher paying) recycling categories.

Properly prepared materials without labels can fetch 10-20% higher prices at many junkshops.

Label Removal Techniques

GLASS CONTAINERS

Glass Jars and Bottles

Soak in warm, soapy water to loosen labels. Use a scrub brush or plastic scraper to remove. For stubborn adhesive, try rubbing alcohol or cooking oil. Remove metal rings from bottle necks.

Clean, label-free glass is more valuable and easier to sort by color.

METAL CANS

Food and Beverage Cans

Most aluminum and steel can labels peel off easily after washing. For stubborn paper labels, soak in warm water. Remove plastic liners from steel cans when possible (often found in food cans).

Label-free aluminum cans are worth more as they don't contaminate the melt.

PLASTIC CONTAINERS

Plastic Bottles and Jugs

Remove plastic sleeve labels completely. Check for adhesive residue. Plastic containers with glued-on paper labels may need soaking. Remove pump mechanisms and non-plastic components.

Plastic labels on plastic containers can contaminate recycling if not separated.

DIFFICULT LABELS

Stubborn Label Removal

For heat-resistant containers, try filling with boiling water to loosen adhesive. Use a hairdryer to warm stubborn labels. Commercial adhesive removers work but rinse thoroughly afterward.

The extra effort for difficult labels pays off in higher quality materials.

GENERAL CONTAINERS

Other Packaging

Remove all non-recyclable components: plastic windows from boxes, wax coatings from paper cartons, foil liners from bags. When in doubt, remove anything that isn't the primary material.

Pure material streams command premium prices in recycling markets.

EXCEPTIONS

When to Leave Labels On

Some labels are designed to burn off during recycling (check with local guidelines). Very small labels may be acceptable. When labels won't come off without damaging the container, it's better to recycle with label than not at all.

Never avoid recycling just because you can't remove a label - check local guidelines.

Label Removal Do's and Don'ts

  • DO remove labels whenever possible to improve material quality
  • DO soak containers to loosen labels and adhesives
  • DO remove all non-recyclable components (plastic spouts, metal rings)
  • DON'T use sharp metal tools that could scratch or damage containers
  • DON'T spend excessive time on labels that won't budge
  • DON'T mix labeled and unlabeled materials in recycling bins

Quick Label Removal Tips

For paper labels: Soak in warm, soapy water for 10-15 minutes before peeling
For plastic labels: Look for perforations or seams to help removal
For adhesive residue: Use cooking oil, rubbing alcohol, or commercial removers
For heat-resistant containers: Fill with boiling water to loosen adhesive

Investing a few extra minutes in label removal can significantly increase your recycling profits.

Remember: Clean, label-free materials are more valuable and easier to recycle!

Proper preparation leads to better quality recycling and higher payouts at junkshops.
